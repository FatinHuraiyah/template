Chemical Engineering as an Academic Discipline

Figure 1 below shows the skills that are required for Chemical Engineering academically:

Figure 1. Chart from Cobb, James and Gary's journal, "The Future of Chemical Engineering". Differences in graduates' reports of engineering skills.

Cobb, Gary and Ranil discuss about the curriculum of Chemical Engineering in their Journal, "The Future of Chemical Engineering":

"Globalization has unquestionably affected the practice of engineering. For one, chemical engineers of today need different skill sets than what was required 15 years ago. Engineering teams are now often multi-national. The ability to work with engineering colleagues from different cultures, with knowledge and sensitivity of multicultural issues, is key. The effects of globalization are resulting in the offshoring of U.S. engineering jobs to overseas competition."

Chemical Engineering is a discipline that is ever-changing following the flow of the modernization. It needs to keep pace with the advancement in technology and to achieve this, collaborations between engineers around the world need to be done. Sharing of information and technology is crucial. Although Chemical Engineering is always advancing, the principles and purpose of Chemical engineering remains the same throughout its existence in the academic world and career world.

In "The Future of Chemical Engineering", Cobb, Gary and Ranil define Chemical Engineering as a discipline that if form by combination of engineering with physical science (chemistry, physics, etc.) and/or life sciences (biology, biochemistry, microbiology, etc.) together with mathematics and economics. It plays a significant role in numerous areas of technology as chemical engineers are the ones who are responsible for the conception and design of processes for the purpose of production, transformation, transportation and proper usage of molecules, chemicals, materials and energy. In a broad term, Chemical Engineering focuses on chemical processes that will convert raw materials into more valuable and useful materials. This in evidently means Chemical Engineering is essential in areas whenever processes involve the physical and chemical transformation; for example the energy and oil industries, the nanotechnology industries, the pharmaceuticals industries and environmental engineering industries.

In "The Future of Chemical Engineering", Cobb, Gary and Ranil also explains that within Chemical Engineering, there are two major subgroups which are the chemical process engineers and the chemical product engineers. Both of them differ in the things that they are creating or handling. Chemical process engineers are responsible in designing, manufacturing and operating of plants and machinery in industrial chemical and related processes while chemical product engineers are responsible in developing new or adapted substances for products ranging from cosmetics to cleaners to pharmaceutical ingredients, to foods and beverages among many other products. Both subgroups must have a comprehensive and computable understanding of both the engineering and scientific principles behind the technological processes in order to achieve their objectives. This is why the curriculum of Chemical Engineering discipline includes the study like applied mathematics, thermodynamics, elementary of chemical processes and fluid mechanics.
